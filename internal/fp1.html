Final Project: Homework 1

By Tom Lucy and Duncan Gans, 10/31/17

Project Name: Campaign Ads in State-Level Races

The collaborator for this project is Michael Michael Franz. He has supplied us with an all-encompassing dataset that provides information on all political advertisements run in the US since the 2000s. To narrow this down slightly, we are choosing to focus on only examples from state-level races, not national or local races.

Political advertisements are a huge part of campaigns and elections, especially since Citizens United, and have become a staple of the American electoral process. Seeing how the nature of political advertisements has changed over time, across different electoral regions, can give us insights into the change of the political atmosphere as a whole.

Columns include: negativity (tone of ad), issue matter, length, cost, market, affiliate station (channel aired on), among others. From these attributes, we have the ability to answer a lot of interesting questions about the data and about political advertisments.

Brainstorming Questions:

  1. How has the tone of ads changed over time?
  2. What issues matter to which voters where?
  3. Do certain tv channels favor one political ideologies ads over the others?
  4. Are certain races (gubernatorial, senate, house) more civil thans others?
  5. What issues do certain parties push?
  6. Is either party more negative?
  7. Are swing states more likely to have negative advertising?
  8. Where is it expensive to have political ads?
  9. In what races are there longer political ads?
  10. Does one party consistenly spend more than the other in ads?
